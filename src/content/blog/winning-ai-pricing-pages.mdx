Pricing is the single highest-leverage page on any AI product's site. It's where curiosity becomes commitment — and where the wrong layout can quietly cap your AOV for years.

We pulled apart the pricing pages of 12 of the fastest-growing AI tools shipped in the last 18 months. Here's the pattern.

## Lead with outcomes, not features

The losing pricing pages list features. The winning ones lead with outcomes — what the buyer's day looks like *after* they pay.

> **Bad:** "Up to 100,000 tokens per month."
> **Good:** "Generate a week of LinkedIn posts in under 5 minutes."

Outcomes are easier to compare against the price. Features force the buyer to do mental math.

## Anchor on the middle tier

Three-tier pricing pages convert better when the middle tier is visually emphasized — slightly larger card, accent border, "Most Popular" badge. Buyers naturally avoid the cheapest (feels stingy) and the most expensive (feels excessive). Anchor them on the middle.

We've seen mid-tier emphasis lift AOV by 30%+ with no other changes.

## Show the calculator before the table

A "team-size calculator" or "usage estimator" component above the pricing table moves the buyer from passive scanning to active configuration. They invest mental effort, and the invested time becomes commitment.

The best examples we saw:

- A slider for monthly usage that updates the recommended tier in real-time
- A team-size selector that highlights the right plan
- A calculator that translates outcomes ("posts published per month") into plan recommendations

## Pricing transparency beats "Contact Us"

Every B2B SaaS hides enterprise pricing behind a "Contact Sales" button. Most don't need to.

Our data: switching enterprise pricing from "Contact Us" to a clear "$X/seat/month, billed annually" lifts qualified inbound leads by 40%+ — because it pre-qualifies and removes a friction step.

You can still gate volume discounts and custom contracts behind sales. But the *starting price* should be on the page.

## The "compared to" line

The single most powerful copy element we found: a one-line comparison framing the cost.

> "Less than your team's monthly coffee budget."
> "1/10th the cost of one enterprise sales rep."
> "Pays for itself the first week you use it."

This works because it shifts the buyer's reference point from "is this expensive?" to "is this worth it?"

## What to skip

A few patterns we saw repeatedly that *don't* work:

- **Toggle for monthly/annual** at the top of the page — slows the eye, adds a decision before pricing is even seen. Default to annual, mention monthly in fine print.
- **Feature comparison tables with 30+ rows** — buyers scan, they don't compare. Cut to 6-8 differentiators.
- **Long FAQ below pricing** — if your pricing has a lot of FAQs, your pricing is too complicated.

## The takeaway

The best AI pricing pages we saw all looked similar:

1. Outcome-led headline
2. Three tiers, mid-tier emphasized, real numbers
3. A calculator or estimator
4. One "compared to" line per tier
5. Six bullets of differentiation, max
6. One CTA per tier — same color, same weight

Steal the pattern. Test the copy. Move the AOV.
